Environmental Impacts

The disaster mobility study will be expaneded to include changes during pandemics. Until recently, disaster recovery in the mobility sector has typically been assessed based on "mobility resiliency" - a measurement of how fast conditions return to "normal" in the wake of a disaster. The problem is that much of society's "normal" behavior is not positive for the environment, nor safe for its inhabitants. Tools for making informed decisions that incorporate environmental, societal and economic impacts are needed as governments and insurers spend billions, and sometimes trillions, during disaster mitagation and recovery. The traditional goal of restoring prior patterns of traffic movement and energy usage are giving way to a desire for transformative "net positive impacts" as massive inflows of spending determine the types of new jobs created during disaster recovery.

Areas for measuring transitions to a positive new normal after disasters:
1. Reduced driving distances, more walking and localized transactions
2. Reduced use of vehicles that emit carbon
3. Reduced use of petrochemicals in production
4. Reduced pollution and carbon emissions
5. Reduced use of vehicles with large passenger compartments
6. Reduced use of facilities in harm's way (near low shorelines or within tight quarters)

Economic and Societal Impacts

From 2013 to 2018, thirty-five storms exceeded a billion dollars in damage and accounted for 80% of direct storm fatalities, causing 14,495 deaths out of 18,111.

From these thirty-five storms, storm mobility stats were generated to identify cities with enough geotagged tweets to track changes in mobility during storms. City boundaries were established using city center lat/lons provided by Google, and data was pre-processed using Python and PostgreSql to display Impact Charts using Seaborn and D3.js. The refinements page includes areas for further investigation.

Updated June 26, 2019
Set human movement maximum speed to 150 kmph. Removed known-bots using a list of 24,578 bots combined from 4 studies. Our 16 storm impacts contained 34 known-bots, which posted 8,601 geotagged tweets.
